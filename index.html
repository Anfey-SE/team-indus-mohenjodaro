DEPTH 1.2M — TOPSOIL

Why this place matters

Long before most of the ancient world had built cities at all, the Indus Valley had already built a planned one. Mohenjo-daro's streets run on a grid, its bricks are cut to a single standard size across the whole settlement, and its drains run covered beneath the street — evidence of coordination and civic planning on a scale historians once assumed was centuries further off.

RISK — Rising groundwater salinity is breaking down the exposed brickwork today. Conservation funding hasn't kept pace with the damage — without sustained investment, evidence that survived 4,500 years could be lost within decades.
DEPTH 2.6M — OCCUPATION LAYER

Core log

2500
BCE

The city is founded along the Indus River, with grid-planned streets and a centralized drainage network — one of the earliest known examples of civic urban design.

1922

Archaeologist R.D. Banerji rediscovers the ruins, revealing a civilization that had been entirely unknown to modern history until this point.

1980

UNESCO designates the site a World Heritage Site, formally recognizing its global archaeological significance.

Today

Salinity, erosion, and underfunded conservation put the surviving structure at risk of irreversible loss within our lifetime.
